Your homepage has one job

Stop designing for everyone. Design for the very next click.

May 2026·5 min read·The Hook

Open your homepage and ask one question: what is the single most useful thing a visitor can do next? If you can't answer in one breath, neither can they. And a confused visitor doesn't email you for clarification. They leave.

Here's the trap most brands in the GCC fall into. The homepage becomes a committee. Sales wants the pitch up top. The founder wants the awards. Someone insists on the full service list because "people need to know everything we do." The result is a page that says everything and asks for nothing. It's a brochure pretending to be a decision.

Stop designing for everyone. Design for the very next click.

Your homepage is a router, not a destination

People don't come to your homepage to admire it. They arrive with a half-formed intent — checking if you're real, comparing you to three other tabs, looking for a price, a portfolio, a phone number — and they want to get to the next thing fast. The homepage's job is to read that intent and route it. It's an intersection, not a parking lot.

Which means the metric that matters isn't time-on-page. It's whether the right person gets to the right next step without thinking. A great homepage is almost invisible. You barely remember it because it didn't make you work.

So design backwards. Pick the one action that, if a visitor takes it, moves your business forward more than any other. Book a call. Request a quote. Start the trial. See the work. That is your primary action. Everything else on the page either points toward it or gets out of its way.

One primary action, ruthlessly chosen

One. Not three of equal weight. When every button shouts, nothing gets heard — psychologists call it choice paralysis, and your bounce rate calls it Tuesday. You're allowed secondary paths, but they must look secondary: quieter colour, smaller, lower on the page. The hierarchy on the screen should mirror the hierarchy in your head.

A test we use at The Hook: the squint test. Blur your eyes and look at the page. The thing that still stands out should be the thing you most want clicked. If it's your logo, or a stock photo of people shaking hands, you've prioritised the wrong thing.

Clarity beats clever, and speed beats both

Above the fold, a visitor should know three things within a few seconds: what you do, who it's for, and what to do next. Plainly. "We help GCC founders set up their company in 10 days" beats "Empowering visionary enterprises to transcend boundaries" every time. Clever taglines that need a second sentence to decode are a tax on attention, and attention is the one budget your visitor controls.

Write the headline as if you're answering a question a real person asked you. Then read it aloud. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it.

And then there's speed, the silent killer. The most beautifully prioritised homepage in the world loses if it takes six seconds to paint on a mid-range phone over patchy mobile data — which is exactly how a lot of your audience in Manama, Riyadh and Jeddah will see it. Every hero video, every carousel, every clever animation is a deposit you're asking the visitor to pay before they've decided you're worth it. Most won't.

The fastest homepage isn't the one with the most features. It's the one with the fewest excuses for the visitor to leave.

The usual suspects

A few mistakes show up again and again. Worth naming so you can hunt them down on your own site this week.

  • The carousel. Auto-rotating sliders are where decisions go to die. You think you're showing five messages. The data on click-through has been brutal for years: almost everyone interacts with the first slide, and the rest may as well not exist. So you've buried four messages and slowed the page to do it. Pick one slide. Delete the carousel.
  • Saying everything. The instinct to list all twelve services on the homepage feels thorough. It reads as noise. Lead with the one or two things you most want to be known for, and let a clean menu carry the rest. Depth lives on inner pages. The homepage is the lobby, not the warehouse.
  • The decorative hero. A full-screen image of a skyline or a smiling team that says nothing about what you do. Pretty, and completely mute. If your hero would work just as well on a competitor's site, it isn't doing its job.
  • The mystery CTA. "Learn more" and "Get started" leading nowhere specific. Tell people exactly what happens on the other side of the click. "Book a free 20-minute strategy call" beats "Get started" because it removes the fear of the unknown.
  • No clear next step at all. The worst one. A page full of information and not a single obvious thing to do. The visitor read everything, felt vaguely interested, and bounced — because you never asked.

How to audit yours in five minutes

Show your homepage to someone outside your company. Give them five seconds, then take it away. Ask: what do we do, and what did you want to click? If they can't answer both, the page is failing at its one job. Then start cutting. Every element you remove that doesn't serve the next click makes the page faster, clearer, and more likely to convert. Subtraction is the most underrated design move there is.

Your homepage has one job. Get the right person to the right next step, fast. Everything else is decoration — and decoration, on a homepage, is just something else in the way. Cut until only the click is left.
