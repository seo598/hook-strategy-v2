AI Search

How to get your brand cited by ChatGPT

A practical framework for GEO, AEO and AIO in 2026 — the new rules for being the answer, not just a blue link.

Jul 2026·8 min read

Search stopped being ten blue links a while ago. Answer engines — ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Google's AI Overviews — now read the web, synthesise it, and hand the user a single confident answer. If your brand isn't in the source set they pull from, you don't rank low; you simply don't exist.

Getting cited comes down to three habits: publish genuinely useful, clearly-structured answers to the questions buyers actually ask; mark them up so machines can parse claims, entities and authorship; and earn corroboration elsewhere so the model trusts you. We call it Search Experience Optimization — and it's the same discipline that wins Google, just pointed at the engines that now sit in front of it.

Performance

The death of the vanity metric

Why we measure pipeline, not impressions — and how to rebuild reporting around revenue.

Jun 2026·5 min read

Impressions, reach and follower count feel like progress because they only ever go up. But a number that can't go down isn't a metric — it's a comfort blanket. The brands that win tie every campaign to something a CFO recognises: qualified pipeline, cost per acquisition, incremental revenue.

Start by deleting the metrics you'd never present to finance. Then rebuild the dashboard backwards from money: which spend created which conversations, which conversations became revenue. It's less flattering and far more useful.

Brand

Designing a hook that scales

The anatomy of an idea that earns attention everywhere — from a 6-second reel to a billboard.

Jun 2026·6 min read

A hook isn't a tagline you bolt on at the end. It's the one tension at the centre of the brand that every execution can pull on — distinctive enough to own, flexible enough to survive a thousand formats without going stale.

Test it the hard way: can a junior designer make ten on-brand things from it without a brief? Can it work as a sound, a motion, a single frame? If the idea only lives in one beautiful hero shot, it isn't a hook — it's a poster.

Social

The three-second rule for the feed

What actually stops the scroll — and what just feels clever in the edit suite.

May 2026·4 min read

The feed is a reflex, not a decision. You have about three seconds before a thumb moves — and most of that budget gets wasted on logos, slow intros and a setup nobody asked for. Open on the payoff, the conflict, or the question. Earn the next three seconds, then the next.

Clever rarely travels. Clear, surprising and human does. If your team laughs in the edit but a stranger wouldn't get it in one pass, it's a private joke, not content.

Web

Your homepage has one job

Stop designing for everyone. Design for the very next click.

May 2026·5 min read

Most homepages try to say everything to everyone and end up saying nothing to anyone. A homepage has one job: get the right person to the right next step — fast, and without making them think.

Decide the single most valuable action on the page, then ruthlessly subordinate everything else to it. Speed, clarity and one obvious path beat a carousel of competing priorities every time.

AI Search

Write the brief with AI, not the post

Where generative tools actually belong in a content workflow — and where they quietly ruin it.

Apr 2026·6 min read

AI is brilliant at the parts of content that are research and structure — clustering questions, mapping intent, pressure-testing an outline, drafting the boring connective tissue. It's weakest at the part that actually matters: a point of view someone will remember.

Use it to get to a sharper brief faster, then write the thing like a human with something to say. The agencies losing the plot are the ones doing it the other way around.

Brand

Naming is positioning in disguise

A name is the first strategic decision you make, not the last creative one.

Apr 2026·5 min read

Teams treat naming as a fun workshop at the end. But a name carries the positioning whether you meant it to or not — it sets expectations, tone and category before anyone reads a word of copy.

Name from the strategy outwards: what do you want to be the obvious choice for, and who for? Get that right and the name does free work for years. Get it wrong and every campaign spends budget explaining itself.
