SEO after AI Overviews

The SERP became an answer. Here is how organic strategy changes — and what stays the same.

Feb 2026·7 min read·The Hook

For twenty years, SEO had one job: get the click. You ranked, the searcher clicked, the click landed on your page, and the page did the rest. That deal is off. The search results page used to be a list of doors. Now, more and more often, it is the room itself. AI Overviews answer the question before anyone reaches for a link, and a large slice of searches end without a single click to anyone.

If your strategy was built to win the click, that strategy now has a hole in it. But before you panic and fire your content team, understand what actually broke and what didn't. Most of what made SEO work still works. The mechanism by which it pays off is what changed.

What didn't change (and never will)

Search intent is still the whole game. A model summarizing the SERP is still answering a query that a human typed because they wanted something. The four intents you already know — informational, navigational, commercial, transactional — didn't dissolve because a language model now sits on top of them. If anything, they got sharper. The AI handles the shallow informational stuff cheaply, which means the queries that still send traffic are the ones with weight behind them: someone comparing, someone deciding, someone ready to act.

Depth still wins. AI Overviews are extractive. They pull from sources that actually said something specific, structured, and checkable. Thin content that restated the obvious never deserved to rank, and now it gets eaten and discarded without even the courtesy of a click. The pages that get cited are the ones with a real answer in them — a number, a process, a comparison, a clear "here is exactly how this works in Bahrain versus Saudi."

And authority still decides who gets believed. Models, like Google before them, lean toward sources that look trustworthy: consistent expertise, real authorship, a brand that shows up again and again in a category. E-E-A-T didn't become a buzzword that died. It became the filter that determines whether you are the source in the answer or just one of the forty pages the model quietly read and ignored.

The old goal was to rank on the page. The new goal is to be the sentence the page is made of.

What changed: from ranking to being cited

Here is the real shift. Visibility and traffic have come apart. You can be the most-cited source in an AI Overview, shaping what millions of people believe about a topic, and receive a fraction of the clicks you used to get for position one. That is not a failure. That is the new top of the funnel — except the top of the funnel is now invisible to your analytics and very visible to your customer.

So you optimize for two things at once. First, being extractable. Structure your content the way an answer engine wants to lift it: a clear question as a heading, a direct answer in the first two sentences, then the depth underneath. Tables for comparisons. Defined terms. Specifics over adjectives. If a model can't cleanly grab your paragraph and drop it into an answer with attribution, someone else's paragraph gets grabbed instead.

Second, you shift weight down the funnel. The top-of-funnel "what is a commercial registration" query is increasingly answered on the SERP itself, click-free. Fine. Let it. Your money was never in that click anyway. It is in the mid and bottom funnel — the comparison pages, the "X versus Y," the pricing realities, the "how do I actually do this in my market" content that a buyer reads right before they pick up the phone. Those queries still convert, still click, and AI Overviews are far weaker at replacing the genuinely decision-grade page.

Brand is the new moat

When the SERP becomes an answer, the thing that survives is the name people search for on purpose. Branded search is the one query type no AI Overview can intercept, because the intent is you. If a buyer in Manama or Riyadh types your company name, the model isn't going to talk them out of it.

This rewires the priority list. Building a brand people seek out by name was always good marketing. Now it is also defensive SEO. The agencies and founders who win the next five years will be the ones who became a known entity — quoted, linked, mentioned, recommended — rather than the ones who merely ranked. Get cited enough, in enough places, and the models start treating you as a default source. That is a flywheel: citation builds authority, authority earns more citation, and brand search climbs as a result.

Practically, for a GCC operator, that means three moves. Publish content specific enough to your market that a generic global page can't compete — local rules, local costs, local process. Earn mentions on the publications and directories your category actually reads. And measure differently: stop staring only at click-through and start tracking citations, branded search volume, and assisted conversions, because those are where the value now hides.

The takeaway is simpler than the panic suggests. AI Overviews didn't kill SEO. They killed lazy SEO. The page that was thin, derivative, and built to farm a click it didn't earn — that page is gone, and good. What replaces it is content good enough to be quoted and a brand strong enough to be searched. Be the source, not the result. The click was always a proxy for being chosen. Now you just have to be chosen on purpose.
