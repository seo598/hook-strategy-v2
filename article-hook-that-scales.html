Designing a hook that scales

The anatomy of an idea that earns attention everywhere — from a six-second reel to a billboard.

Jun 2026·6 min read·The Hook

Most brands don't have a hook. They have a tagline they paid too much for, a logo lockup, and a deck full of adjectives. Then they wonder why the work feels different every time it leaves the building — a reel that sounds like one company, a billboard that sounds like another, a sales email that sounds like nobody at all.

The fix isn't more guidelines. It's a hook that travels. An idea sharp enough to survive six seconds on a phone and big enough to hold up on a hoarding on the Manama–Riyadh road. That's the thing worth designing. Here's how to build one that scales instead of one that just sits in a brand book.

A hook is a tension, not a tagline

Let's separate two things people constantly confuse.

A tagline is a line of copy. It's an output. "Just do it." "Think different." Words, fixed, on a page.

A hook is the central tension your brand owns — the unresolved gap between two true things that makes people lean in. It's not written; it's felt. A tagline is one expression of the hook. The hook itself can produce a thousand expressions and still feel like one voice.

Take the underdog idea behind a lot of great work: we're smaller, so we try harder. That tension — small versus mighty — is the hook. It can become a headline, a product feature, a hiring ad, a customer-service script, a TikTok. The tagline is just the day it wore a suit.

So before you brief a designer or a content team, answer one question: what is the tension only we can credibly hold? Not the benefit. The tension. "Premium coffee" is a benefit. "Specialty coffee shouldn't require a passport to your wallet" is a hook — there's a fight in it, a villain, a side to take.

If your idea has no opponent, it's not a hook. It's a description. Descriptions don't scale because there's nothing to push against.

The four tests for whether it scales

Once you think you've got the tension, put it through these. An idea that scales passes all four. Most "big ideas" fail at least two.

1. The compression test

Can it survive six seconds with the sound off? If the idea only works once you've explained it for thirty seconds, it isn't a hook — it's a briefing. Strip it to a single visual gesture, a single line, a single contrast. If a viewer scrolling at speed can't feel the tension before the caption loads, it won't travel on the formats where attention is thinnest.

2. The expansion test

Now go the other way. Can the same idea fill a 48-sheet billboard, a three-minute brand film, and a year of social with no repetition? A hook that scales is generative — it spawns more ideas than you can use. If you've drained it after one campaign, you built a stunt, not a system. The test: can your junior team brief themselves off it without calling you? If yes, it's deep enough.

3. The format-shift test

Move it across channels and watch what happens to the meaning. The words can change. The tone can flex. The tension must stay intact. A reel might be funny, the billboard stark, the case study earnest — but a Bahraini founder should recognise the same brand underneath all three. If the idea only works as a video, or only as a headline, it's tied to a format. Hooks are format-agnostic. Executions aren't.

4. The Arabic test

This one is non-negotiable in the GCC, and it's where a lot of imported ideas die. Does the tension hold in Arabic and English? Not the literal translation — the feeling. Wordplay rarely survives the crossing; tension almost always does, because tension is human before it's linguistic. If your hook depends on an English pun, you've built something that breaks at the border. Build the tension first, let each language find its own sharpest expression of it.

Distinctiveness and flexibility: the two-axis grid

Here's the trap. Teams optimise for one of two things and lose the other.

Chase distinctiveness alone and you get a brand so locked-down it can only say one thing one way. Memorable, sure. But rigid. It can't react to a moment, can't enter a new category, can't let a smart 22-year-old on the social team riff without "breaking guidelines."

Chase flexibility alone and you get a brand that can do anything and therefore means nothing. Every post is on-trend and off-brand. By December nobody remembers a single thing you said.

A hook that scales is distinctive at the core and flexible at the edges. The tension is fixed. The expression is free.

Picture it as concentric rings. At the centre: the tension — immovable. Around it: a tone of voice and a few visual codes that bend but don't snap. At the outer edge: executions that change weekly, by platform, by audience, by language. The discipline is knowing which ring you're touching. Most brand erosion happens because someone "freshened up" the centre when they should have only been playing at the edge.

This is also how you brief freelancers and junior creatives without micromanaging. You don't hand them a list of rules. You hand them the tension and the edges they're allowed to push. Good people do better work inside a sharp constraint than inside a vague freedom.

The takeaway

Stop opening the brief with "we need a tagline." Open it with "what's the fight?" Find the tension only you can credibly own, then pressure-test it: six seconds silent, a year of content, every channel, both languages. If it survives all four, you don't have a line — you have a hook. And a hook, unlike a tagline, gets sharper the more places you take it. That's the whole point. Great ideas don't just take bait. They scale it.
