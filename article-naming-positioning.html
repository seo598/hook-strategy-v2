Naming is positioning in disguise

A name is the first strategic decision you make, not the last creative one.

Apr 2026·5 min read·The Hook

Most teams treat naming as the bow on top. The strategy is done, the deck is signed off, the logo is in the works, and someone says "right, we just need a name." A shortlist gets passed around. People vote for the one that sounds nice on a Tuesday. The name that wins is usually the one nobody hates.

That is the wrong way round. A name is not the last creative flourish. It is the first strategic commitment you make in public. Every time someone says it, types it, or hears it, your positioning gets reinforced or quietly undermined. You don't get to add a footnote. The name does the arguing for you, in rooms you will never be in.

The name is carrying your positioning whether you planned it or not

Positioning is the answer to one question: in the customer's head, what are you, and who are you better than? A good name compresses that answer into a word or two. A bad name forces you to spend money explaining what the name should have said for free.

Think about what a name has to do in the GCC specifically. It has to work in English and Arabic. It has to survive being said over a bad phone line by a receptionist. It has to read cleanly on a CR document and on a billboard on Sheikh Zayed Road. It has to mean nothing embarrassing in Khaleeji and nothing worse in Egyptian or Levantine. That is before it does any positioning work at all.

So when people say "it's just a name," they're admitting they haven't thought about how hard the name is working. It sets your category, your price tier, and your personality before you've said a word about any of them. A clinic called "Precision" and a clinic called "Hayah" are selling different promises. Neither owner may have intended that. The name decided anyway.

Name from the strategy outwards, not from the thesaurus inwards

Here is the order that actually works. Most teams do it backwards.

  • Start with the position. One sentence: who it's for, what it replaces, why it's different. If you can't write that sentence, no name will save you.
  • Decide what the name must do. Should it claim the category outright? Signal premium? Feel local and trusted, or global and ambitious? You cannot optimise for all of these. Pick.
  • Generate against the brief, not against vibes. A name is a candidate, not a poem. Judge it on whether it advances the position, not whether the founder's cousin likes it.
  • Pressure-test it out loud, bilingually, with strangers. Say it on a call. Put it in a sentence a customer would use. Watch where people stumble.

Naming from strategy outwards means the hard thinking happens before the wordplay. By the time you're playing with sounds, you already know what the name has to win. That is the difference between a name that earns its keep and one you'll quietly rebrand away from in three years.

The rough types, and what each one buys you

You're choosing a trade, not just a word. Broadly:

  • Descriptive (Bahrain Business Setup). Instantly clear, terrible at standing out, hard to own legally. Great for SEO, weak for a brand you want to charge a premium on.
  • Suggestive (evokes the benefit without spelling it out). The sweet spot for most companies. Says enough to position, leaves room to grow. "The Hook" is one of these — it tells you we're about ideas with pull, without naming a service.
  • Abstract or invented (a coined word). Maximum ownability, maximum cost. You're funding all the meaning yourself. Fine if you have the budget to teach the market. Reckless if you don't.
  • Founder or place names. Trust and heritage, especially in the Gulf where a family name carries weight. The cost is flexibility — your name now travels everywhere the business goes, good day or bad.

None of these is correct in the abstract. The right type is the one that matches the position you wrote in step one. A challenger brand built on an invented word is paying for distinctiveness it could have rented more cheaply. A premium firm hiding behind a flat descriptive name is leaving money on the table every time it quotes.

The cost of a name that needs explaining

Here is the test that cuts through every shortlist: does the name need a sentence after it?

If you have to explain the name every time you say it, you are paying a tax on every conversation for the life of the company.

That tax is real. It shows up in the extra line on every email. The pause before the punchline in a pitch. The mispronunciation that gets repeated until the wrong version sticks. The domain you couldn't get, so now you're "gettheapp" something. Multiply that friction across thousands of touchpoints and you've spent a marketing budget patching a decision you made in an afternoon.

A name that needs explaining isn't always wrong. Sometimes the explanation is the story, and the story is the point. But that has to be a choice, made knowing the bill. Most names that need explaining weren't chosen for a reason. They were chosen because they sounded good and nobody asked the harder question.

So ask it early. Before the logo, before the deck, before the domain panic. Write the position, then find the word that already agrees with it. Naming isn't the creative afterthought at the end of the process. It's the first time your strategy has to survive contact with a real person's mouth. Get it right and the name sells for you, quietly, forever. Get it wrong and you'll be explaining yourself until you rebrand.
